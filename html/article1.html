Software engineers foresee the future
by Julius Cylien

Artificial intelligence is the most important technological shift since the smartphone, maybe since the internet itself. Yet every time AI comes up, the loudest reactions tend to be fear, anger, or a form of resistance. I’ve always wondered why. When people complain about AI, I don’t hear concerns about safety or ethics, I hear frustration at progress. A process which brings us closer to curing diseases like cancer, accelerating scientific discovery, and even building systems like universal basic income (known as UBI) that could fundamentally reduce poverty.

Artificial intelligence will not solve these problems on its own. Software engineers will.

Machine learning engineers, systems engineers, hardware engineers, and researchers are the people turning abstract ideas into tools that scale. They are the force multiplier behind nearly every modern breakthrough. Scientists don’t move faster without software.

Medicine doesn’t advance without computation. Governments don’t function at scale without digital infrastructure. Other fields absolutely matter but none of them scale without software. I was introduced to programming recently during my senior year of high school, and it completely changed how I view different aspects of the world. With the help of tools like ChatGPT and Claude acting as mentors available every hour of the day, I went from confusion to building my first backend API in a short time frame. That experience made something obvious to me, software engineering isn't just about writing code, it's about unlocking human potential and creativity. When engineers build the right abstractions and tools, they allow millions of people in other fields to work faster, think bigger and solve harder problems.

One of the most controversial ideas tied to automation is universal basic income, UBI is a system where people receive income regardless of employment, and since the rise of generative AI, the topic has moved from theory into mainstream political and economic discussion. I don't think it will happen anytime soon but I do believe it’s inevitable. As automation replaces more forms of labor, society will be forced to choose between restructuring technology or restructuring how people survive.

Banning AI production would be the worst possible decision. Innovation doesn’t stop at borders, if one country slows down, another accelerates. Automation, robotics, and artificial intelligence will continue to advance whether we’re ready or not. The real question is whether we build systems that protect people during the transition or let progress widen inequality.

Many people argue that computer science fields are becoming oversaturated. I see the opposite. We need engineers. More people are building infrastructure, tools, robotics, and intelligent systems. Nearly everything that defines modern life like communication, medicine, research, education, and creativity all exist because software and hardware engineers made it possible. As technology continues to advance, it won’t eliminate creativity, it will amplify it, no more wasting weeks on a singular drawing. The impact won’t just be generational, it will be global.

This belief became more personal to me recently. I wasn’t able to celebrate Christmas the way I usually do, and while it didn’t bother me much, it made me think about kids and families who live under constant financial pressure. No child should grow up in a world where family time disappears because survival comes first. No one should have to choose between being present and being paid.

That’s why I want AI to take jobs, NOT because people are disposable but because their time isn’t. Human life shouldn't optimize around labor. It should be optimized around learning, creativity, connection, and meaning. Automation gives us the chance to redesign society around those values, if we’re willing to make difficult transitions to get there. This won’t happen quickly. It may take decades, centuries, or longer. But every line of code written today pushes that future a little closer. A future where fewer people worry about their next paycheck. Where kids grow up with stability. Where technology serves humanity, instead the other way around.

The future is worth the effort. It’s worth the sacrifice...
